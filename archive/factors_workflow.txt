Smart Predictive Pool Maintenance System

Prediction 1 - Chlorine Safety Forecast
How It Works
Factors:
The system learns the normal chlorine behaviour of each pool using historical chlorine readings, pH values, temperature, dosing activity, and environmental conditions.
Chlorine Readings Over Time
	•	The model studies how chlorine normally changes between visits. 
	•	It learns whether chlorine usually drops slowly or rapidly for a specific pool. 
	•	If chlorine starts decreasing faster than normal, the system identifies abnormal chemical consumption. 
pH Readings Over Time
	•	The model checks pH because chlorine effectiveness depends heavily on pH balance. 
	•	High pH reduces chlorine efficiency even when chlorine readings appear normal. 
	•	Continuous pH instability increases the probability of unsafe water conditions. 
Water Temperature
	•	Higher temperatures increase chlorine loss and biological activity. 
	•	The model learns that hotter pools usually consume chlorine faster. 
	•	Temperature is cross-checked with chlorine decay behaviour to improve prediction accuracy. 
pH-Chlorine Effectiveness Index
	•	The system combines chlorine level and pH together to estimate actual disinfecting effectiveness. 
	•	This prevents false safety assumptions caused by acceptable chlorine readings with poor pH balance. 
Chlorine Decay Rate
	•	The model calculates how quickly chlorine disappears between visits. 
	•	Faster decay indicates increased contamination, environmental exposure, or unstable chemistry. 
	•	If the decay trend keeps increasing, the chlorine risk prediction becomes more critical. 
Dosing Hours and Dosing Percentage
	•	These values show how much effort the dosing system is using to maintain chlorine levels. 
	•	If chlorine continues decreasing despite high dosing activity, the model detects abnormal demand or inefficient treatment. 
Chlorine Dosing Pump Flow Rate
	•	Helps estimate the actual chlorine entering the system. 
	•	If chlorine levels remain unstable while pump flow remains normal, the system interprets contamination or environmental exposure as the likely cause. 
Pool Surface Area
	•	Larger pools receive more sunlight exposure. 
	•	Increased sunlight accelerates chlorine degradation and evaporation. 
Pool Type
	•	Different pool environments behave differently. 
	•	Outdoor, heated, public, and indoor pools each have different normal chlorine behaviour patterns. 
Vegetation Nearby
	•	Leaves, pollen, and organic matter increase chlorine consumption. 
	•	Pools surrounded by vegetation usually experience faster chlorine decay. 
Type of Enclosure
	•	Open pools are exposed to sunlight, wind, and external contamination. 
	•	Enclosed pools maintain more stable chemical conditions. 
Final Prediction Logic
	•	The system combines all factors together to estimate future chlorine behaviour. 
	•	The model analyses relationships between chlorine decay, pH balance, temperature, and dosing activity. 
	•	If abnormal chlorine behaviour is detected, the system generates a chlorine safety alert.
Prediction 2 - Filter Backwash and Replacement
How It Works
Factors:
The system studies filtration behaviour and learns how contamination normally accumulates in each pool over time.
Turbidity Readings Over Time
	•	The model monitors how cloudy the water becomes between visits. 
	•	Slow turbidity increase indicates normal filter loading. 
	•	Rapid increase indicates abnormal contamination or filtration problems. 
Pump Flow Rate
	•	Flow rate measures how efficiently water is circulating through the system. 
	•	Reduced flow together with rising turbidity strongly suggests filter blockage. 
Turbidity Growth Rate
	•	Measures how quickly contamination is increasing. 
	•	Faster growth than normal indicates excessive debris or filtration inefficiency. 
Hydraulic Efficiency
	•	Compares actual flow performance against expected system behaviour. 
	•	Lower efficiency indicates circulation degradation or equipment stress. 
Turnover Time
	•	Measures how effectively pool water is circulating and being filtered. 
	•	Increasing turnover time means water is moving more slowly through the filtration system. 
Days Since Last Filter Wash
	•	Tracks how long the filter has operated since the previous cleaning cycle. 
	•	Longer operation periods increase the probability of filter loading. 
Filter Wash Time Over Multiple Events
	•	Increasing wash duration may indicate severe contamination buildup or filter wear. 
	•	Long-term wash behaviour helps estimate filter condition. 

Filter Diameter and Number of Filters
	•	Larger or multiple filters can handle more contamination before performance drops. 
	•	These values help define the normal filtration capacity for each pool. 
Daily Filtration Hours
	•	Increasing filtration runtime may indicate the system is compensating for declining efficiency. 
Vegetation Nearby
	•	Leaves and organic debris increase contamination loading on filters. 
Floor Type
	•	Grass and mixed flooring introduce additional dirt and particles into the pool. 
Pool Type
	•	Public and outdoor pools usually experience higher contamination levels. 
Final Prediction Logic
	•	The system compares current filtration behaviour against historical patterns. 
	•	Rising turbidity together with reduced flow and worsening turnover time indicates filtration degradation. 
	•	The model predicts when filter cleaning or replacement may become necessary and generates maintenance alerts.

pH Dosing Percentage and Chlorine Dosing Percentage

Maintenance Cycle Monitoring
Prediction 5 - Equipment Maintenance Cycle
How It Works
The system tracks the operating hours and performance trends of each piece of equipment per pool. It learns the normal working behaviour of each equipment over time and predicts when maintenance or replacement is needed before a breakdown happens.
1)Main Circulation Pump
Factors: Daily Filtration Hours
	•	The main pump runs every day to circulate water through the filter system.
	•	Every hour it runs adds wear to the pump mechanism.
	•	The system accumulates these hours across every visit to build a total operating hours count per pool.
	•	When cumulative hours approach the service threshold, a maintenance alert is generated.
Formula 
Cumulative Pump Operating Hours = Sum of (Daily Filtration Hours at Visit 1 + Visit 2 + Visit 3 ... Visit N) per pool, reset after each recorded service

2)Main Pump Flow Rate
	•	This is the designed flow rate of the pump - what it should deliver at full health.
	•	The system compares actual flow performance against this designed value over time.
	•	If flow rate is consistently dropping while filtration hours remain normal, the pump is losing efficiency.
	•	Combined with cumulative hours, this confirms whether the pump needs service.



Formula: 
Hydraulic Efficiency = (Actual Pump Flow Rate at Visit N) ÷ (Designed Pump Flow Rate) × 100
	•	Number of Motors tells the system whether the pool has one pump or multiple pumps.
	•	A single pump pool has no backup - any degradation signal is treated as urgent.
	•	A multi pump pool has lower urgency and more time to schedule service.
No formula - static value recorded once per pool. Used to determine alert priority level. Single motor = urgent alert. Multiple motors = standard alert
Final Prediction Logic
	•	The system tracks cumulative pump operating hours per pool continuously.
	•	Hydraulic efficiency is calculated at every visit and compared against the designed flow rate baseline.
	•	When cumulative hours approach the service threshold and hydraulic efficiency is also declining, a pump maintenance alert fires.
	•	Single pump pools receive escalated priority alerts compared to multi pump pools.

3)pH Dosing Pump
Factors:
pH Dosing Hours
	•	The pH dosing pump runs independently from the main pump and wears at its own rate.
	•	The system tracks cumulative dosing hours separately for this pump per pool.
	•	When total hours approach the service threshold, a pH pump specific alert fires.
Formula: 
Cumulative pH Dosing Hours = Sum of (pH Dosing Hours at Visit 1 + Visit 2 + Visit 3 ... Visit N) per pool, reset after each recorded service

4)pH Dosing Percentage
	•	This value shows what fraction of the pump's maximum capacity is being used each visit.
	•	A healthy pump maintains a stable dosing percentage to achieve consistent pH results.
	•	If the dosing percentage keeps rising visit after visit while pH results stay the same or worsen, the pump is delivering less chemical per hour than it should.
	•	The system identifies this upward trend as an early wear signal before the pump fails completely.
Formula: 
pH Pump Wear Signal = (pH Dosing Percentage at Visit N − pH Dosing Percentage at Visit N−1) ÷ Days Between Visits

5)pH Pump Flow Rate
	•	The designed flow rate of the pH pump is the baseline the system compares against.
	•	The system calculates how much of the original pump capacity remains at each visit.
	•	If this percentage keeps dropping visit after visit, degradation is confirmed.
	•	Combined with rising dosing percentage, this gives a strong confirmation that the pump needs servicing.
Formula: 
pH Pump Hydraulic Efficiency = (Actual pH Pump Flow Rate at Visit N) ÷ (Designed pH Pump Flow Rate) × 100
Final Prediction Logic
	•	The system combines cumulative pH dosing hours, dosing percentage trend, and hydraulic efficiency together.
	•	Rising cumulative hours alongside consistently increasing dosing percentage and declining hydraulic efficiency confirms the pH pump is degrading.
	•	A pH pump service alert is generated early enough to schedule maintenance without disrupting pool operations.
Chlorine Dosing Pump
Factors:
1)Chlorine Dosing Hours
	•	Tracked independently from the pH pump and main pump because each degrades at a different rate.
	•	The system builds a separate cumulative hours counter for the chlorine pump per pool.
	•	When total hours approach the service threshold, a chlorine pump specific alert fires.

Formula: Cumulative Chlorine Dosing Hours = Sum of (Chlorine Dosing Hours at Visit 1 + Visit 2 + Visit 3 ... Visit N) per pool, reset after each recorded service

2)Chlorine Dosing Percentage
	•	Same principle as pH dosing percentage but applied to the chlorine pump.
	•	If the chlorine dosing percentage keeps rising over visits but chlorine levels do not improve or keep dropping, the pump is losing efficiency.
	•	The system tracks this upward trend as the primary wear signal for this pump.

Formula: Chlorine Pump Wear Signal = (Chlorine Dosing Percentage at Visit N − Chlorine Dosing Percentage at Visit N−1) ÷ Days Between Visits

3)Chlorine Pump Flow Rate
	•	The designed flow rate acts as the performance baseline for the chlorine pump.
	•	The system calculates how much of the original pump capacity remains at each visit.
	•	Declining hydraulic efficiency alongside rising dosing percentage confirms pump degradation.
	•	Combined with cumulative hours, this gives the system a complete picture of chlorine pump health.

Formula: Chlorine Pump Hydraulic Efficiency = (Actual Chlorine Pump Flow Rate at Visit N) ÷ (Designed Chlorine Pump Flow Rate) × 100
Final Prediction Logic
	•	The system combines cumulative chlorine dosing hours, dosing percentage trend, and hydraulic efficiency together.
	•	Continuously rising dosing effort alongside declining hydraulic efficiency and the same or worse chlorine result confirms the pump is degrading.
	•	A chlorine pump service alert fires before performance drops to a level that affects water safety.

Filter Media
Factors:
1)Filter Wash Time
	•	Every time the filter is backwashed the duration of that wash is recorded.
	•	A healthy filter takes a consistent amount of time to wash each cycle.
	•	As the filter media ages it becomes harder to clean and each wash takes progressively longer.
	•	The system tracks this wash duration across every recorded visit per pool and calculates the trend.
Formula: Filter Wash Time Trend = (Filter Wash Time at Visit N − Filter Wash Time at Visit N−1) ÷ Days Between Visits
2)Date of Visit
	•	Used to calculate exactly how many days pass between each backwash event per pool.
	•	If the interval between backwashes keeps getting shorter over time, the filter is clogging faster than before.
	•	A shrinking backwash interval alongside increasing wash time is a strong combined signal of accelerating filter degradation.
Formula:
 Backwash Interval = Date of Current Wash Event − Date of Previous Wash Event per pool
3)Number of Filters and Filter Diameter
	•	These define the total physical filtration capacity of the pool.
	•	A pool with a large or multiple filters takes longer to reach its degradation threshold than a pool with a single small filter.
	•	The system uses these values to set the correct maintenance and replacement thresholds individually per pool rather than applying a generic threshold to every pool.
	•	Static values recorded once per pool. Used together to define total filtration capacity and set correct maintenance thresholds per pool.
Final Prediction Logic
	•	The system monitors wash time trend and backwash interval trend together.
	•	If wash time is increasing visit after visit and the gap between washes is getting shorter, the filter media is degrading.
	•	The system projects the wash time trend forward and predicts when it will exceed the acceptable limit for that pool.
	•	A filter replacement alert fires with enough lead time to plan and carry out the replacement before filtration performance becomes unacceptable.
